# Phase 3: Supply Lines Cut

**Course Topic:** Economic Security

**Time Period:** Day 6-10 of crisis (60 minutes real-time)

---

## Context

The cyber attack that began five days ago has cascaded beyond infrastructure and information into the region's economic foundations. With power still intermittent, communications unreliable, and public trust shaken by Phase 2's misinformation campaigns, the economic fabric of Southwest Virginia is unraveling.

Three major coal mines have gone idle after cyber damage to their control systems left ventilation, drainage, and safety monitoring offline. Over 2,000 miners are suddenly without income, and the coalfield counties that depend on mining revenue face an immediate fiscal crisis. Coal ash containment systems are running on manual — one failure could cause an environmental catastrophe.

Food City's regional distribution center is operating on backup power with only 48 hours of inventory remaining. The panic buying that began in Phase 2 never fully subsided, and rural stores are empty. Pharmacies can't verify prescriptions electronically — dialysis patients at Carilion facilities face critically low supplies, and opioid recovery patients are missing daily medication doses. Gas stations across the region are empty; school buses have stopped running and emergency vehicles are on reserve fuel.

Over 200 small businesses can't process card payments. Payroll has been missed for 5,000+ workers. Downtown Blacksburg and Roanoke storefronts are shuttering. A cash-only economy is creating chaos at every register. Meanwhile, FEMA's regional office has ruled that a cyber attack doesn't qualify for Stafford Act disaster relief, freezing $50M in federal aid pending legal determination.

The region faces a defining question: how do you stabilize an economy when the digital systems it depends on are still compromised? And as resources dwindle, the urban-rural divide sharpens — do you concentrate aid where population density is highest, or prioritize the isolated communities that have the fewest alternatives?

## Crisis Engine Analysis

### What just changed unexpectedly?
The extended cyber disruption has crossed a threshold from infrastructure inconvenience into full economic collapse. Systems that appeared to be recovering (power, communications) haven't recovered enough to support economic activity. The interdependencies between digital payment systems, supply chains, healthcare delivery, and employment have created a cascading failure that no single sector can address alone.

### Which system is now stressed?
- [x] Economy: Mass business closures, payment system failure, unemployment spike
- [x] Supply Chain: Food, fuel, and medical supplies critically low
- [x] Healthcare: Prescription systems down, dialysis supplies depleted
- [x] Infrastructure: Power intermittent, communications unreliable
- [x] Governance: Federal disaster qualification disputed, tax/utility revenue suspended

The economic system is the primary stressed system, but unlike a typical recession, this collapse is driven by digital infrastructure failure — meaning traditional economic interventions (stimulus, loans) have limited effect until the underlying systems are restored.

### Who experiences immediate harm?
- [x] Workers: 2,000+ miners idle, 5,000+ missed payroll, small business employees
- [x] Vulnerable populations: Dialysis patients, opioid recovery patients, elderly on fixed income
- [x] Rural communities: Empty stores, no fuel, no public transit alternatives
- [x] Small businesses: Can't process payments, facing permanent closure
- [x] Young people: VT students transferring, young families planning to relocate

### What decision must be made within limited time?
**Urban vs. Rural Resource Allocation:** With limited supplies arriving, teams must decide whether to concentrate distribution in population centers (more people served per dollar) or prioritize rural communities (more vulnerable, fewer alternatives). This decision will cascade into Phase 5's healthcare and environmental outcomes.

**Deadline:** Food City's warehouse has 48 hours of inventory. Fuel reserves for emergency vehicles are critically low. Dialysis patients need supplies within hours.

### What future risk is created by this decision?
- **If urban-focused:** Rural communities collapse entirely, environmental monitoring gaps widen, brain drain accelerates
- **If rural-focused:** Urban population centers face shortages, business recovery delayed, political backlash
- **If federal aid pursued aggressively:** Legal battle takes weeks, but success unlocks $50M
- **If federal aid abandoned:** Must fund everything locally, budgets depleted for later phases
- **If coal mines ignored:** Ventilation failure risks mine collapse; coal ash containment failure causes environmental disaster

### Who makes the decision, and who is left out?
- **Decision makers:** Governor's Liaison (state resources), Montgomery County (local allocation), EMA (emergency distribution), Food City (supply chain)
- **Stakeholders excluded:** Rural communities without representatives at the table, miners without union representation, undocumented workers in service industry
- **Why this matters:** The people most affected by economic collapse have the least political power to direct the response

## Constraints

### Time Constraints
- Phase duration: 60 minutes real-time = Days 6-10 simulated
- Critical decision deadline: ~0:10 (fuel and food distribution)
- Escalation triggers: 0:10-0:20 depending on severity
- Food City warehouse: 48-hour supply window

### Budget Constraints
- Budgets carry over from Phase 2 end state
- Economic stabilization requires significant spending (stimulus, loans, convoys)
- Some teams have very limited budgets (Zion $1.56M, Sequoia $2.5M)
- Federal aid ($50M) frozen — pursuing it costs nothing but takes time
- Cost-splitting available for collaborative actions

### Resource Constraints
- Fuel: Critically low, must be escorted
- Food: 48hr warehouse supply, rural stores empty
- Medical supplies: Dialysis and prescription verification systems down
- Power: Intermittent, priorities must be set for restoration
- Transportation: Limited fuel means limited logistics capacity

### Information Constraints
- Known: Economic collapse is widespread, federal aid disputed, supply levels critical
- Unknown: How long until payment systems restored, whether federal aid will be approved, full scope of mine damage
- Unreliable: Reports of looting (may be exaggerated), fuel availability rumors

### Political/Legal Constraints
- Stafford Act interpretation: Cyber attack as "disaster" is legally untested
- Price control authority: Governor can implement but risks market distortions
- Environmental compliance: Suspension saves costs but creates future liability
- Emergency powers: How far can state go in directing private business operations?

### Ethical Boundaries
- Urban vs. rural allocation: Population density efficiency vs. equity of access
- Price controls: Market freedom vs. preventing exploitation
- Environmental compliance suspension: Short-term relief vs. long-term contamination risk
- Coal mine workers: Economic lifeline of region vs. environmental transition

## Technology Layer

### How Technology Failure Drives Economic Collapse
- Payment processing systems depend on network connectivity destroyed by cyber attack
- Supply chain management is entirely digital — manual workarounds are slow and error-prone
- Prescription verification requires real-time database access that no longer exists
- Mine safety systems are cyber-physical — can't just "switch to manual" safely
- Banking systems can't clear transactions without digital infrastructure

### How Technology Creates Asymmetry
- Urban areas have more cellular coverage alternatives; rural areas have none
- Large businesses have IT teams for workarounds; small businesses have nothing
- Hospital systems can get priority network access; rural pharmacies cannot
- Card-only businesses are locked out; cash-heavy businesses (limited) continue

### How Technology Enables Response
- Satellite communications can bypass damaged terrestrial networks
- Mobile payment alternatives (if cellular works) can replace card processing
- Emergency communication networks can coordinate supply distribution
- Economic impact modeling can support federal disaster qualification

## Facilitator Notes

### Key Learning Objectives
1. Understand how digital infrastructure failure cascades into economic collapse
2. Experience resource allocation dilemmas under scarcity (urban vs. rural equity)
3. Recognize interdependencies between supply chain, healthcare, energy, and employment
4. Navigate the tension between emergency economic intervention and market principles
5. Understand how federal disaster policy creates gaps for novel threat types (cyber attacks)

### Common Participant Mistakes
- **Stimulus without infrastructure:** Spending money when payment systems don't work
- **Ignoring coal communities:** Focusing on urban areas while coalfield counties collapse
- **Piecemeal response:** Addressing food OR fuel OR medicine instead of seeing supply chain holistically
- **Federal aid tunnel vision:** Waiting for federal money instead of acting with available resources
- **Neglecting morale:** Focusing on logistics while community despair drives exodus

### Discussion Points for Debrief
- How did the digital dependency of the economy surprise you?
- What made the urban vs. rural allocation decision difficult?
- How did budget constraints force creative collaboration?
- Should cyber attacks qualify for federal disaster relief?
- What role did community organizations play vs. government agencies?

### Adjustment Levers
- **Easier:** Federal aid approved quickly, fuel convoy arrives early, payment systems partially restored
- **Harder:** Second cyber attack hits banking, mine ventilation fails, Food City warehouse loses backup power
- **More ethical tension:** Coal miners protest at Food City distribution site, pharmacy diversion for black market
- **More coordination pressure:** Add cross-team dependencies for supply convoy routes
