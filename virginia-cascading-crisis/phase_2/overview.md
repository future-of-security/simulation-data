# Phase 2: Rumors Spread

**Course Topic:** Data, Privacy, Surveillance, & Misinformation

**Time Period:** Day 3-5 of crisis (60 minutes real-time)

---

## Context

With hospital systems still partially down and the cyber attack only partially contained, a new front opens. The 500 patient records published on the dark web in Phase 1 have spread across social media. Deepfake videos of local officials surface — one shows the Governor's liaison allegedly saying "coalfield counties aren't a priority." AI-generated news articles claiming a government cover-up go viral on Facebook groups. Churches and community centers become both hubs of trusted information and vectors for well-meaning but inaccurate rumors. The stolen VT research data raises national security concerns as intelligence agencies suspect a foreign state actor.

Two days after the initial attack, trust is fragile. The communities that rallied through church networks and volunteer efforts now face a more insidious threat: weaponized information. The same social bonds that helped people survive the first 48 hours — tight-knit congregations, neighborhood group chats, family phone trees — are now being exploited to spread convincing lies. And the real data breach gives every rumor a kernel of truth that makes it harder to debunk.

---

## Crisis Engine Analysis

### What just changed unexpectedly?
The cyber attack's data theft has been weaponized into an information warfare campaign. Stolen patient records are organized into a searchable database. Deepfake technology is being used to fabricate statements by public officials. The attack has shifted from infrastructure disruption to social disruption — exploiting the region's trust networks.

### Which system is now stressed?
- [x] Information: Deepfakes, misinformation campaigns, weaponized data
- [x] Health: Patients refusing care due to privacy fears
- [x] Governance: Public trust in officials collapsing
- [ ] Infrastructure: Partially recovered from Phase 1
- [x] Economy: Not primary but class-action lawsuits emerging

The information ecosystem is the primary stressed system. In a region where word-of-mouth and church networks are the dominant information channels, sophisticated disinformation exploits these trust-based systems with devastating effectiveness.

### Who experiences immediate harm?
- [x] Individuals: Patients whose HIV, mental health, and opioid treatment records are exposed
- [x] Vulnerable communities: Hispanic residents afraid of surveillance, opioid patients avoiding treatment
- [x] Institutions: Carilion (liability), government officials (deepfake attacks), law enforcement (trust loss)
- [x] Cross-border populations: National security implications from foreign state attribution

### What decision must be made within limited time?
**Transparency vs. Control:** Teams must decide how much to reveal about the data breach, surveillance activities, and foreign state attribution. Full transparency may cause panic; controlled messaging may be seen as another cover-up.

**Deadline:** The deepfake video is spreading exponentially — every hour of delay doubles its reach. Patient data is being downloaded faster than it can be taken down.

### What future risk is created by this decision?
- **If full transparency:** Panic about data breach, political fallout from surveillance revelation, diplomatic complications from attribution leak
- **If controlled messaging:** Accused of cover-up (again), deepfakes fill information vacuum, community trust collapses further
- **If surveillance is defended:** Civil liberties backlash, Hispanic community withdraws entirely
- **If surveillance is condemned:** Potential intelligence about attackers is lost

### Who makes the decision, and who is left out?
- **Decision makers:** Governor's Liaison, media, law enforcement, federal coordinators
- **Stakeholders excluded:** Patients whose data was leaked, Hispanic community members, rural residents without internet who only hear rumors
- **Why this matters:** The people most harmed by the data breach and surveillance have the least power to shape the response

## Constraints

### Time Constraints
- Phase duration: 60 minutes real-time = Days 3-5 simulated
- Critical decision deadline: ~0:10 (deepfake and fake alerts spreading fastest)
- Escalation trigger: ~0:15 (data breach notification deadline)

### Budget Constraints
- Budgets carry over from Phase 1 end state
- Legal/PR crisis response costs premium rates
- Credit monitoring for 500 patients: $500K minimum
- Counter-disinformation campaigns require media buys

### Resource Constraints
- Deepfake detection expertise: Limited (VT AI lab primary resource)
- Legal counsel: High demand, limited availability
- Multilingual communicators: Critical need, very few available
- Platform takedown requests: Slow response from social media companies

### Information Constraints
- Known: Patient data is public, deepfake exists, surveillance was deployed
- Unknown: Full scope of data breach, source of deepfakes, foreign attribution certainty
- Unreliable: Social media narratives, anonymous tips, leaked intelligence

### Political/Legal Constraints
- HIPAA breach notification: 60-day deadline but public pressure demands faster
- Fourth Amendment concerns around warrantless surveillance
- Classified intelligence complicates public attribution
- First Amendment protections for social media content

### Ethical Boundaries
- Patient privacy already violated — response must not cause further harm
- Surveillance evidence may be valuable but was illegally obtained
- Foreign attribution could inflame xenophobia in community
- Counter-disinformation must not become censorship

## Technology Layer

### How AI/Technology Accelerates Harm
- Deepfake generation makes fabricated video indistinguishable from real
- Algorithmic amplification spreads misinformation faster than corrections
- Searchable database of stolen records maximizes privacy harm
- Spoofed emergency alerts exploit trusted communication channels
- Cell-site simulators capture data from innocent bystanders

### How AI/Technology Creates Asymmetry
- Creating deepfakes takes minutes; debunking takes hours
- One leak reaches millions; corrections reach thousands
- Surveillance technology available to law enforcement but oversight lags behind
- Social media platforms respond slowly to takedown requests
- Rural communities have fewer tools to verify information

### How AI/Technology Enables Response
- AI analysis can identify deepfake artifacts and prove manipulation
- Digital forensics can trace disinformation campaign coordination
- Automated monitoring can detect emerging false narratives early
- Trusted communication networks (church phone trees) remain analog and resistant to digital manipulation

## Facilitator Notes

### Key Learning Objectives
1. Understand how deepfakes and AI-generated content weaponize trust
2. Experience tension between transparency and information control
3. Recognize how data breaches cause cascading social harms beyond the initial theft
4. See how surveillance technology creates civil liberties dilemmas even when used with good intentions
5. Understand the unique information dynamics of tight-knit rural communities

### Common Participant Mistakes
- **Platform fixation:** Focus on social media takedowns while ignoring offline rumor networks
- **Top-down messaging:** Issue press releases instead of using trusted community channels
- **Ignoring victims:** Focus on the disinformation fight while patients suffer in silence
- **Defending surveillance:** Justify warrantless surveillance because it produced useful intelligence
- **Attribution rush:** Share unconfirmed intelligence about foreign actors, causing panic

### Discussion Points for Debrief
- How did the deepfake change your trust in information, even after debunking?
- What made community information channels both a strength and vulnerability?
- How should law enforcement balance surveillance power with civil liberties?
- Who bears the cost of the data breach — the institution or the patients?
- How does foreign state attribution change the nature of the crisis?

### Adjustment Levers
- **Easier:** Deepfake has obvious tells, platform cooperation is fast, attribution is clear
- **Harder:** Multiple deepfakes surface, platform refuses takedown, attribution is ambiguous
- **More ethical tension:** Patient suicide linked to data exposure, surveillance caught a real threat
- **More coordination pressure:** Add cross-team dependencies for counter-disinformation
