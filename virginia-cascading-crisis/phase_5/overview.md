# Phase 5: Hidden Damage

**Course Topic:** Health, Environmental, & Biosecurity

**Time Period:** Day 16-25 of crisis (60 minutes real-time)

---

## Context

Phase 4 held the line on governance and civil unrest — but the costs were invisible. In the rush to manage protests and deepfakes, critical monitoring systems stayed offline. Environmental sensors were never restarted. Rural health infrastructure remained fragmented. The coalfields were stabilized politically, but nobody checked what was happening underneath.

Now the hidden damage surfaces all at once.

Carilion's rural clinics — already understaffed before the cyber attack — are running on fumes. Dialysis patients who survived two weeks of disruption are now at immediate risk: four rural clinics are offline, and the transport gaps cannot hold. Medication supplies for opioid recovery patients are running short, and overdose calls have spiked 40% in three days. The healthcare system that barely held together during Phase 4 is now facing collapse on multiple fronts simultaneously.

The environmental picture is just as alarming. Environmental sensors restored after the attack are sending readings nobody is ready for. A coal ash pond in Wise County shows potential structural issues before a major rain event. Three rural water systems — operated on manual backup for two weeks — show elevated contamination markers in preliminary tests. A coal processing facility that restarted without monitoring now sits at the center of a respiratory illness cluster affecting 47 residents in a coal community.

These are not rumor or political narrative problems. They are evidence-based crises — but the evidence is incomplete, the decisions are irreversible in both directions, and the public is watching. Premature advisories could trigger panic and supply crises. Delayed action could cause preventable deaths and long-term health damage that haunts the region for decades.

Teams must triage healthcare while simultaneously assessing environmental risks and managing communication about what is known, what is unknown, and what is being done.

## Crisis Engine Analysis

### What just changed unexpectedly?

Hidden environmental and health damage that was masked by Phase 4's governance crisis has surfaced simultaneously. The region is dealing with health triage + environmental risk + communication demands all at once — with no additional resources and exhausted teams.

### Which system is now stressed?

- [x] Health: dialysis, MOUD, hospital surge capacity, rural clinic collapse
- [x] Environment: coal ash integrity, water contamination, air quality, mine restart
- [x] Information: incomplete data driving high-stakes decisions; premature disclosure risk
- [ ] Infrastructure
- [ ] Governance (stabilized but fragile)

### Who experiences immediate harm?

- [x] Individuals: dialysis patients, opioid recovery patients, residents drinking contaminated water, coal community with respiratory illness
- [x] Vulnerable communities: rural elderly in hollows without transport, low-income families without safe water alternatives
- [x] Institutions: Carilion (capacity crisis), DEQ (evidence and authority gaps), faith/community networks (overwhelmed)
- [ ] Cross-border populations

### What decision must be made within limited time?

**Triage vs. transparency vs. precaution.** Teams must decide which health emergencies to prioritize with limited resources, how quickly to issue environmental advisories with incomplete data, and how to communicate risks without triggering panic that worsens supply chain and community stability.

**Deadline:** Dialysis patients require treatment every 48 hours — deaths are preventable only if transport is arranged within the first 20 minutes of play. Coal ash structural window closes before the forecast rainfall event within the first 15 minutes.

### What future risk is created by this decision?

- Delayed dialysis transport: preventable deaths, federal scrutiny, legal exposure
- Premature water advisory: panic buying, supply chain disruption, loss of DEQ credibility
- Missed environmental enforcement: long-term contamination, mine restart liability
- Over-medicalizing the respiratory cluster without evidence: creates biosecurity panic; under-responding: outbreak grows
- Insufficient MOUD support: community relapse crisis with multigenerational consequences

### Who makes the decision, and who is left out?

- Decision makers: DEQ, Carilion, Governor's Liaison, EMA, VT Research
- Stakeholders excluded: rural residents without transportation, opioid recovery community without political voice, coal workers dependent on the mine reopening
- Why this matters: the excluded groups bear the greatest harm from both action and inaction, and their exclusion from decision-making will shape Phase 6 community trust.

## Constraints

### Time Constraints
- Phase duration: 60 minutes real-time = Days 16-25 simulated
- Dialysis patient window: 0:15 before preventable deaths
- Coal ash window: 0:10 before storm makes stabilization ineffective
- MOUD clinic collapse: 0:20 if no emergency dispensing protocol

### Budget Constraints
- Budgets carry over from Phase 4 end state
- Most teams have reduced budgets after Phase 4 expenditure
- Environmental stabilization (coal ash) is expensive ($500K); requires trade-offs
- Health actions carry high social returns but significant cost

### Resource Constraints
- Carilion staff exhausted from Phase 4; surge capacity near limit
- DEQ has limited field staff for simultaneous coal ash + water + air monitoring
- Transport capacity limited; VDOT corridors still recovering from Phase 4 protest disruption
- Community trust (faith networks, United Way) is high but organizational bandwidth strained

### Information Constraints
- Known: dialysis patients at risk, coal ash sensors alarming, water tests preliminary, overdose spike, respiratory cluster
- Unknown: coal ash structural timeline, water contamination source/extent, respiratory illness cause, MOUD supply timeline
- Unreliable: social media claims about mine collapse, water "poison," outbreak origins

### Political/Legal Constraints
- Precautionary water advisory triggers mandatory bottled water distribution (costly)
- Mine shutdown order requires Governor approval and creates economic backlash in coalfields
- Federal CDC involvement triggers notification requirements and may alarm public
- Environmental enforcement decisions have long-term legal liability implications
