# Phase 6: Mountain Resilience

**Course Topic:** Disaster Management

**Time Period:** Day 26-35 of crisis (60 minutes real-time)

---

## Context

Phase 5 laid bare the hidden costs of the cascading crisis — and forced the region to confront them in real time. The dialysis emergency escalated into preventable deaths and a federal inquiry. Coal ash seepage contaminated the Clinch River before stabilization could take effect. But the region also showed what coordinated response looks like: the MOUD crisis was averted through peer recovery networks, the respiratory cluster was identified before it became a biosecurity event, and the water advisory was issued correctly, lifted cleanly, and turned into a model for public health communication.

Now the acute phase is over. The National Guard has stood down. The cyber attack's technical vectors have been contained. But Southwest Virginia is not the same region it was 25 days ago.

Three dialysis patients are dead. Federal investigators are in Wise County. The Clinch River has a do-not-use advisory that may last months. Four hundred coal miners are out of work pending environmental review. Eight hundred opioid recovery patients narrowly avoided a medication crisis that would have set the region back a decade. Twelve thousand residents learned their water was contaminated — and then learned it wasn't — and now don't know what to believe.

The question is no longer "how do we survive?" It is "what do we become?"

Federal disaster assistance is contested. FEMA's Stafford Act was designed for hurricanes and earthquakes — does a cyber attack that cascaded into environmental and health crises qualify? The answer determines whether the region receives hundreds of millions in recovery aid or must rebuild with state and local resources alone. Meanwhile, the communities that held together through mutual aid and faith networks are asking whether rebuilding the old systems — the ones that failed — is the right goal. Young professionals are leaving. Coal communities see the mine halt as another betrayal. Roanoke institutions want to modernize; coalfield towns want to survive.

Teams must navigate federal disaster policy, allocate scarce recovery resources across competing needs, decide what to rebuild versus what to transform, and do it all while maintaining the fragile community trust that was earned — and damaged — over the previous five phases.

## Crisis Engine Analysis

### What just changed unexpectedly?

The transition from crisis response to recovery reveals that the hardest decisions are not behind the region — they are ahead. Federal disaster qualification is uncertain, recovery funding is finite, and every allocation decision will create winners and losers in a region already divided along urban-rural, institutional-community, and generational lines.

### Which system is now stressed?

- [x] Governance: federal disaster policy, state-local authority, recovery planning legitimacy
- [x] Economy: coal transition, workforce displacement, small business collapse, brain drain
- [x] Infrastructure: what to rebuild, what to harden, what to replace entirely
- [x] Health: long-term care systems, environmental health monitoring, behavioral health
- [ ] Information (stabilized but trust fragile)

### Who experiences immediate harm?

- [x] Individuals: displaced coal workers, small business owners, patients who lost continuity of care, families of dialysis fatalities
- [x] Vulnerable communities: coalfield towns facing permanent mine closure, elderly without rebuilt services, opioid recovery patients whose support networks are exhausted
- [x] Institutions: Carilion (legal exposure from deaths), county governments (unfunded recovery mandates), school systems (enrollment decline)
- [x] Cross-border populations: Tennessee border communities dependent on shared infrastructure; downstream Clinch River users in multiple states

### What decision must be made within limited time?

**Rebuild vs. transform.** Federal disaster application has a filing window. Recovery funding — if it comes — must be allocated before political attention moves on. Communities must decide whether to restore pre-crisis systems or invest in resilient alternatives. Every dollar spent rebuilding the old way is a dollar not spent on transformation. Every dollar spent on transformation is a dollar that doesn't help people who need services restored *now*.

**Deadline:** FEMA preliminary damage assessment must be filed within the first 15 minutes of play. Recovery resource allocation framework must be established by 0:25 or communities begin making uncoordinated decisions that fragment the response.

### What future risk is created by this decision?

- Rebuilding old systems: restores service quickly but locks in the same vulnerabilities that caused the cascading failure
- Investing in transformation: builds long-term resilience but leaves gaps in immediate service delivery; risks alienating communities that need help now
- Prioritizing urban recovery: efficient per-capita but deepens rural abandonment
- Prioritizing rural recovery: addresses equity but stretches limited resources thin
- Accepting federal strings: unlocks funding but cedes local control over recovery priorities
- Rejecting federal conditions: preserves autonomy but may leave the region without resources to recover at all

### Who makes the decision, and who is left out?

- Decision makers: FEMA, Governor's office, county governments, Carilion, economic development authorities
- Stakeholders excluded: coal workers whose jobs depend on mine restart decisions, small business owners without political voice, youth who will inherit the consequences, environmental justice communities downstream of coal ash
- Why this matters: recovery decisions made in the next 10 days will shape the region for 30 years. The people most affected have the least say.

## Constraints

### Time Constraints
- Phase duration: 60 minutes real-time = Days 26-35 simulated
- FEMA preliminary damage assessment: 0:15 before automatic denial
- Healthcare accountability response: 0:20 before lawsuit filing
- EPA remediation preemption: 0:20 before federal consent order
- Infrastructure federal comment: 0:20 before window closes

### Budget Constraints
- Budgets and trust are reset to sector-appropriate starting values for the recovery phase — reflecting fresh institutional mandates and new funding streams, not depleted crisis accounts
- Recovery actions are expensive — federal cost-share is available only if FEMA application succeeds
- Environmental remediation costs dwarf available local budgets; federal and state support is essential
- Cost multiplier: teams with trust below 5 face 25% surcharge on all recovery actions

### Resource Constraints
- Personnel exhausted after 25-day crisis; institutional burnout is real
- Carilion staff at breaking point; cannot sustain emergency surge indefinitely
- Community volunteers (faith networks, mutual aid) have been operating beyond capacity
- DEQ field staff limited; cannot simultaneously monitor coal ash, water systems, and air quality long-term
- Political capital is finite; every recovery decision spends it

### Information Constraints
- Known: extent of physical damage, number of affected residents, Phase 5 environmental data, federal disaster criteria
- Unknown: whether FEMA will classify cyber-cascading crisis as qualifying event, long-term health impacts of water contamination exposure, coal ash remediation timeline, true scope of economic displacement
- Unreliable: cost estimates for recovery (historically underestimated by 40-60%), federal funding timelines, mine restart feasibility

### Political/Legal Constraints
- Federal disaster declaration requires demonstrating that the crisis exceeds state/local capacity — politically sensitive given Virginia's resources
- Dialysis wrongful death exposure creates legal constraints on Carilion's public communications
- Coal mine restart is tied to environmental review completion — economic pressure vs. regulatory obligation
- Recovery funding allocation will be scrutinized by state legislature; urban-rural equity is politically charged
- EPA can preempt state environmental authority if remediation is deemed inadequate

### Ethical Boundaries
- Recovery funding must not be conditioned on communities accepting unwanted development or infrastructure
- Coal worker retraining must be voluntary and provide genuine economic alternatives, not performative programs
- Environmental remediation timelines must prioritize community health over cost optimization
- Dialysis families deserve accountability and compensation, not just institutional reputation management
- Recovery planning must include meaningful community participation, not token consultation

## Technology Layer

### How AI/Technology Accelerates Harm
- Algorithmic resource allocation tools may optimize for efficiency metrics that disadvantage rural and low-density communities
- AI-generated economic projections could justify abandoning coalfield investment based on population trends alone
- Automated federal compliance systems may disqualify non-standard disaster types (cyber-cascading) that don't fit historical templates
- Social media amplifies out-migration signals, accelerating brain drain through network effects

### How AI/Technology Creates Asymmetry
- Roanoke institutions have data science capacity to build compelling federal applications; coalfield counties do not
- Carilion's legal team can use AI for document review and strategy; plaintiff families cannot
- Economic development tools favor regions with existing digital infrastructure — the communities that need recovery most are least visible in the data
- Federal funding algorithms weight population density, systematically disadvantaging rural applicants

### How AI/Technology Enables Response
- Virginia Tech's resilience modeling can identify highest-impact infrastructure investments with limited data
- Telehealth platforms formalized during Phase 5 can become permanent rural healthcare delivery
- Environmental sensor networks with AI-driven anomaly detection can prevent future hidden damage scenarios
- Digital case management systems can track individual recovery needs and prevent people from falling through gaps
- Broadband expansion enables remote work, reducing brain drain by allowing young professionals to stay in the region

## Cascading Effects

### From Previous Phases
- **Phase 1 cyber response decisions** → determined the technical debt now embedded in restored systems; quick-fix restorations are vulnerable to repeat attack
- **Phase 2 transparency choices** → shaped whether communities trust official recovery communications or view them as spin
- **Phase 3 urban-rural allocation** → established the equity baseline; communities that felt shortchanged in Phase 3 will scrutinize Phase 6 allocation intensely
- **Phase 4 governance tensions** → unresolved political grievances resurface during recovery funding debates; coalfield communities may reject state-led recovery frameworks
- **Phase 5 health and environmental outcomes** → dialysis deaths create legal and moral obligations; environmental data shapes remediation scope and cost; MOUD and water response successes provide models for sustainable programs

## Facilitator Notes

### Key Learning Objectives
- **Disaster management is not crisis response.** Recovery requires different skills, timescales, and stakeholder engagement than emergency response. The transition from "saving lives" to "rebuilding futures" is where many real-world responses fail.
- **Federal disaster policy shapes recovery.** Understanding how disaster declarations work — and their limitations for non-traditional threats like cyber attacks — is essential for modern security professionals.
- **Equity in recovery allocation.** Every dollar allocated is a values statement. Students must confront the tension between efficiency (per-capita optimization) and equity (serving the most vulnerable and historically underinvested communities).
- **Rebuild vs. transform is the defining dilemma.** The pressure to restore normalcy quickly conflicts with the opportunity to build something better. This tension is present in every real-world disaster recovery.
- **Accountability and trust are inseparable.** Institutions that avoid accountability for failures (dialysis deaths, environmental damage) may preserve short-term stability but destroy the trust needed for long-term recovery.

### Common Participant Mistakes
- **Treating recovery as a technical problem:** Focusing on infrastructure repair while ignoring community engagement, economic transition, and accountability. Recovery is fundamentally a governance and equity challenge.
- **Ignoring the FEMA deadline:** The federal disaster application is the single highest-leverage action in Phase 6. Teams that treat it as bureaucratic paperwork rather than a strategic priority lose access to the recovery's primary funding source.
- **Urban-first allocation by default:** Teams instinctively allocate resources where the largest populations are, not where the greatest need or vulnerability lies. This reproduces the Phase 3 mistake that created Phase 5's hidden damage.
- **Avoiding the accountability question:** Teams may try to defer or avoid addressing the dialysis deaths and environmental damage. Families and communities will not wait, and avoidance escalates into legal and political crises.
- **Assuming recovery is the end:** Teams may make decisions without considering long-term consequences because "the simulation is ending." In reality, recovery decisions lock in outcomes for decades.

### Discussion Points for Debrief
- How did decisions in Phases 1-5 constrain or enable recovery options in Phase 6?
- Who was included in recovery planning, and who was excluded? How did that affect outcomes?
- What is the right balance between restoring services quickly and investing in long-term resilience?
- Should a cyber attack that cascades into health and environmental crises qualify for federal disaster assistance? What are the policy implications?
- How would this recovery look different if the crisis had hit an urban area with more resources and redundancy?
- What role should affected communities play in deciding how recovery resources are allocated?
- How do you hold institutions accountable for failures while still needing those institutions to lead recovery?

### Adjustment Levers
If the simulation is too easy or too hard, facilitators can:
- **Increase difficulty:** Introduce a competing disaster (hurricane season, another cyber incident) that diverts federal attention; reveal additional environmental contamination; accelerate the out-migration timeline
- **Decrease difficulty:** Pre-approve FEMA qualification; provide additional state emergency funding; extend deadlines for escalation triggers
- **Increase complexity:** Introduce a community referendum on recovery priorities; add a whistleblower revealing institutional negligence; surface a second coal ash pond with unknown status
- **Shift focus:** Emphasize the economic transition angle by adding a major employer relocation offer; emphasize the health angle by revealing long-term contamination exposure data; emphasize the governance angle by introducing a state legislative oversight hearing
